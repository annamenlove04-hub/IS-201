The Sweet Essence of France

French gastronomy is celebrated worldwide for its precision, technique, and rich history. In 2010, the "gastronomic meal of the French" was declared an Intangible Cultural Heritage by UNESCO. At the heart of this gastronomic tradition is the art of baking—specifically, Viennoiserie and Pâtisserie.

Essential Classifications of French Baking

To understand French baking, one must learn the three traditional pillars of the craft. Here is a breakdown of the core categories and their iconic examples:

  1. Viennoiserie (Breakfast Pastries)
    • Croissant: A buttery, flaky, crescent-shaped roll made of laminated yeast dough.
    • Pain au Chocolat: The same laminated dough wrapped around two bars of dark chocolate.
    • Pain aux Raisins: A spiral-shaped pastry filled with pastry cream and raisins.
  2. Pâtisseries (Fine Confections)
    • Macarons: Sweet meringue-based confections made with egg whites, icing sugar, granulated sugar, and almond meal.
    • Éclairs: Oblong pastries made with choux dough filled with chocolate or coffee pastry cream and topped with fondant icing.
    • Tarte Tatin: An upside-down pastry in which the fruit (usually apples) is caramelized in butter and sugar before the tart is baked.
  3. Boulangerie (Artisanal Breads)
    • Baguette de Tradition: The classic long thin loaf, strictly regulated by French law to contain only flour, water, yeast, and salt.
    • Pain de Campagne: A rustic sourdough loaf made from a mix of wheat and rye flours.

Did You Know?

The word "Viennoiserie" literally translates to "things from Vienna." The croissant was actually inspired by the Austrian Kipferl and was popularized in France during the 1830s when Austrian bakers opened shops in Paris!
